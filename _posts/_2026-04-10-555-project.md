## Introduction

The 555 timer is an eight-pin integrated circuit (IC) which has been in production since 1972. It has a variety of uses, particularly in timing, delay, pulse generation, and oscillators. While many believe its name is derived from the three internal 5 kΩ resistors, its creator Hans Camenzind stated in an interview that the name was purely arbitrary and created by the marketing manager.

The 555 timer has two main usage configurations: monostable and astable. In the monostable configuration, the IC outputs a pulse when the input drops below 1/3 of the supply voltage; the output pulse width depends on the resistor and capacitor values used. In the astable configuration, the IC outputs a repeating pulse signal, with pulse timing depending on the resistor and capacitor values used.

The objectives of this project are to explore, measure, and document the 555 timer IC uses in both monostable and astable mode. Then, a unique simple project is conducted using a 555 timer — in this case, a traffic light timer circuit. 555 timers are estimated to be one of the most used ICs and are incredibly versatile; this project is important in developing a strong understanding of the basic operation of the chip, which can be applied in many ways and is vital for an Electrical Engineer to be familiar with.

## Theory

### 555 Timer Overview

The 555 timer internal circuit functions as follows:

**Voltage Divider**: Between the positive supply voltage $$V_{cc}$$ and ground GND is a voltage divider made of three 5 kΩ resistors. The CONTROL input at pin 5 is attached between the upper two resistors, allowing one of the reference voltages to be controlled by an external signal. When CONTROL is not driven, the voltage divider creates an upper reference signal of $$2/3\, V_{cc}$$ into the threshold comparator, and a lower reference signal of $$1/3\, V_{cc}$$ into the trigger comparator.

**Threshold comparator**: This comparator's positive input is connected to the THRESHOLD input at pin 6, and its negative input is connected to the voltage divider's upper reference signal.

**Trigger comparator**: This comparator's positive input is connected to the voltage divider's lower reference signal, and its negative input is connected to the TRIGGER input at pin 2.

**Latch**: A set-reset (SR) latch is controlled by the two comparators and stores the current state of the timer. The RESET pin at pin 4 allows a signal to override the two comparator inputs, allowing the latch — and therefore the entire timer — to be reset if needed.

**Output**: The output passes through a push-pull output driver to supply the output current.

**Discharge**: The output also controls a transistor, which connects the DISCHARGE pin at pin 7 to ground through an electronic switch.

### Monostable Mode of Operation

The circuit is stable when the output is LOW, and unstable when the output is HIGH. When a negative trigger is applied to pin 2, the trigger comparator activates, setting the SR latch and driving the output HIGH. This also releases the discharge transistor at pin 7, causing an external capacitor to begin charging. When the capacitor charges sufficiently, it triggers the threshold comparator, resetting the SR latch and pulling the output LOW, which turns on the discharge transistor and drains the capacitor. The output pulse width is governed by:

$$t = 1.1 \times R \times C \tag{1}$$

where $$R$$ and $$C$$ are the respective resistance and capacitance values in the circuit.

### Astable Mode of Operation

In astable mode, the configuration is unstable, alternating between HIGH and LOW outputs. Starting with an uncharged capacitor, the cycle proceeds as follows:

1. The capacitor's voltage starts at 0 V ($$< 1/3\, V_{cc}$$), which activates the trigger comparator, causing the SR latch to change state, triggering a HIGH output and cutting off the discharge transistor.
2. The capacitor begins charging through $$R_1$$ and $$R_2$$ since they are no longer shorted to ground through the discharge transistor.
3. When the capacitor charges to $$2/3\, V_{cc}$$, the threshold comparator triggers, switching the SR latch, sending a LOW output, and switching on the discharge transistor.
4. The capacitor begins discharging through $$R_2$$ and the discharge transistor.
5. The cycle repeats once the capacitor's voltage falls back below $$1/3\, V_{cc}$$.

![Astable circuit diagram](/assets/posts/555-project/2_Theory/Astable_Diagram.png)
*Figure 4: Generalized circuit for astable operation.*

Since the capacitor charges through both $$R_1$$ and $$R_2$$ but discharges only through $$R_2$$, the high and low pulse intervals are:

$$t_{high} = \ln(2) \cdot (R_1 + R_2) \cdot C \tag{2}$$

$$t_{low} = \ln(2) \cdot R_2 \cdot C \tag{3}$$

The total frequency $$f$$ is:

$$f = \frac{1}{t_{high} + t_{low}} = \frac{1}{\ln(2) \cdot (R_1 + 2R_2) \cdot C} \tag{4}$$

And the duty cycle $$D$$ is:

$$D\,(\%) = \frac{t_{high}}{t_{high} + t_{low}} \times 100 = \frac{R_1 + R_2}{R_1 + 2R_2} \times 100 \tag{5}$$

## Experimental Procedure

The application chosen was a model traffic light circuit using two 555 timers. It consists of three LEDs (red, yellow, and green): the green LED turns on and stays on for some time, then the yellow LED briefly illuminates, and finally the red LED turns on for approximately the same duration as the green. The cycle then repeats.

The circuit functions by using two 555 timers, with the second timer powered directly from the output of the first — meaning it only operates when the first timer's output is HIGH. The red LED is wired to the first timer's output in an inverted fashion so it illuminates only when that output is LOW. The yellow LED is connected to the discharge pin of the second timer and lights during its discharge phase, while the green LED turns on whenever the second timer's output is HIGH.

When power is first applied, the first timer's output goes HIGH immediately since the voltage at its trigger pin (pin 2) starts below $$1/3\, V_{cc}$$. The red LED stays off, but the second timer receives power and its output goes HIGH, turning the green LED on. As the second timer's capacitor charges toward $$2/3\, V_{cc}$$, the second timer's output flips LOW and the discharge pin activates, switching off the green LED and turning on the yellow. The first timer's capacitor then reaches its own $$2/3\, V_{cc}$$ threshold first, cutting power to the second timer entirely — extinguishing the yellow LED and pulling the first timer's output LOW, which lights the red LED. Once the first timer's capacitor discharges back below $$1/3\, V_{cc}$$, the output goes HIGH again, the second timer restarts, and the whole sequence repeats.

The simulation consistently overestimated the expected timing values, with $$t_{green}$$ and $$t_{yellow}$$ deviating by over 22% and 25% respectively, likely due to idealized component models in LTSpice that do not account for real-world tolerances, or a simulation error. The experimental results were considerably more accurate, with $$t_{green}$$ and $$t_{red}$$ falling within 2–4% of their expected values. The most notable remaining discrepancy is in $$t_{yellow}$$, which measured 3.3 s against the expected 4 s (17.5% error), likely attributable to component tolerances or breadboard parasitic resistances affecting the RC time constant of that phase. Overall, the experimental implementation validates the core circuit design.

## Conclusion

The 555 timer IC is an eight-pin integrated circuit that uses an internal voltage divider, two comparators, and an SR latch to control its output. It was explored in three configurations: monostable, astable, and a practical traffic light application.

The traffic light application chained two 555 timers together in astable mode, with the second timer powered from the first timer's output, coordinating the sequencing of green, yellow, and red LEDs. The simulation showed discrepancies of up to 25% from expected values, likely due to idealized component models, though the experimental results were much more accurate (within 2–17%) and the circuit functioned correctly in its intended sequence.

Several improvements could be made in future work. Using potentiometers in place of fixed resistors would allow fine-tuning of timing values to reduce rounding error. For the traffic light circuit, measuring voltage across the current-limiting resistors rather than directly at the output pins would provide a cleaner signal. Investigating and resolving the anomaly in astable configuration 1 more carefully during setup could also have identified and corrected the source of error.

To conclude, the experiments demonstrated that the 555 timer is a versatile and reliable IC, capable of producing accurate timing results across multiple configurations. The insights gained from both the successes and discrepancies observed here provide a strong foundation for applying the 555 timer in more complex real-world circuits.
